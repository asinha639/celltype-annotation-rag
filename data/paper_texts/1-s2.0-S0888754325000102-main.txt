ABSTRACT
Background: Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) is a typical autoimmune disease characterized by a complex 
pathogenesis and a strong genetic predisposition. The study of inflammatory response in SLE monocytes is not 
very clear, and exploring the inflammatory factors of monocytes is beneficial to discover new diagnostic targets.
Results: Using scRNA-seq technology, we obtained the quantitative changes in circulating immune cells and 
various cellular immune metabolic profiles between SLE patients and healthy volunteers. A significant increase 
in monocytes was observed in peripheral blood of SLE patients. Flow cytometry was employed to validate the 
types and quantities of circulating immune cells in SLE, corroborating the scRNA-seq results. Monocyte highly 
expressed IRF1 (interferon regulatory factor 1) in SLE. Previous research proves that IRF1 is widely involved in 
immune regulation and inflammatory response, and can promote the transcription of a variety of pro- 
inflammatory cytokines. Additionally, Inflammatory factors secreted by monocytes in serum were measured. 
The results demonstrated a significant upregulation of IFN-γ, TNF-
α , IL-2, IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, IL-1β in the sera of SLE 
patients compared to healthy controls.
Conclusion: Our results demonstrate upregulation of monocyte inflammation in circulating immune cells in SLE 
patients and expands the current understanding of circulating immune cells in SLE.
Our study provides a blueprint for future exploration of SLE monocytes, revealing the pathogenesis and inventing 
new immunotherapies.
1. Introduction
Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is an autoimmune disease 
characterized by the overactivation of the immune system and the 
abnormal deposition of antigen-antibody complexes [1,2 ], leading to 
dysfunction in various tissues and organs such as the skin, joints, kid -
neys, lungs, cardiovascular system, etc. The pathogenesis of SLE remains 
unclear [3], and the disease course is prone to recurrence [4 ]. Studies 
have shown that SLE is associated with genetic background, environ -
mental factors, and estrogen levels [ 5], with incidence rates ranging 
from 20 to 200 cases per 100,000 people. Higher incidence rates are 
observed among African, Hispanic, and Asian populations [6
]. Clini-
cally, the male-to-female ratio of SLE patients is 1:9 [ 7], with a pre -
dominance of female patients, particularly those of childbearing age [8]. 
The complex pathogenesis of SLE is primarily linked to decreased 
immune tolerance, immune complex deposition, antibody production, 
activation of immune complexes in target tissues, and inflammatory 
responses.
Despite significant advancements in immunosuppressive therapies, 
including glucocorticoids, cyclophosphamide (CYC), and mycopheno -
late mofetil (MMF), the early diagnosis and treatment of SLE remain 
challenging due to various factors.
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) is an unbiased analytical 
method that directly examines gene expression at the single-cell level 
[9], enabling the analysis of cellular heterogeneity, cell type definition, 
cell state determination, and dynamic cell transitions [10 ]. This tech -
nology holds significant potential in exploring the pathogenesis of 
various human diseases [
11,12]. Immune cells play a crucial role in 
innate immune responses by producing various inflammatory factors 
such as interferons (IFNs). Therefore, this study aims to investigate the

2. Materials and methods
2.1. Peripheral blood sample collection
Peripheral blood samples were collected from systemic lupus ery -
thematosus (SLE) patients (age: 27 ± 6 years, female, n = 7, experi-
mental group) and healthy volunteers (age: 26 ± 3 years, female, n = 6, 
control group) at Shenzhen People’s Hospital. This study was approved 
by the Medical Ethics Committee of Shenzhen People’s Hospital 
(Approval No.LL-KY-2022058) and conducted in accordance with the 
principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. All participants provided 
written informed consent.
Exclusion Criteria for SLE Patients: 
• Presence of other autoimmune diseases.
• HIV/AIDS or other immunodeficiency conditions.
• History of high-dose glucocorticoids (≥1 mg/kg/day) or other 
immunosuppressive agents.
• Pregnancy.
• SLE patients with SLEDAI score were all in the active stage, all are 
active periods. (see Table 1 for details).
2.2. Single-cell RNA sequences
Isolation of PBMC samples, 10× genomics scRNA-seq library prep -
aration, scRNA-seq data processing as described previously [16], batch 
effect correction, cellular subtyping and annotation as well as pathway 
enrichment analyses, cellular Phone DB as described in Xu et al. [17].
2.3. Flow cytometry
Fresh blood samples were collected from SLE patients (n = 6) and 
healthy controls (n = 4) using EDTA anticoagulant tubes. The samples 
were centrifuged at 500G/min, and the supernatant was discarded. The 
remaining pellet was treated with erythrocyte lysis buffer to erythro -
cyte, followed by centrifugation. The resulting pellet was resuspended 
and counted. The blank control (without anti-bodies), homotypic con
-
trol (homotypic antibodies), and sample tubes were incubated with 
target antibodies CD19 (BioLegend 302,207), CD3 (BioLegend 
317,305), CD4 (BioLegend 300,529), CD8 (BioLegend 344,713), and 
CD66B+ (BioLegend 305,517). percp-cy5.5 (560,525, BD, USA), MS 
anti-cd11b PE (557,397, BD, USA), and APC rat anti-mouse f4/80 
(566,787, BD, USA) at 4 
◦C for 30 min. After staining, the samples were 
washed with PBS and transferred to flow cytometry tubes for analysis 
using a Canon II (BD) flow cytometer. Voltage adjustments were made 
using unstained controls, and compensation was adjusted with single- 
stained controls. Data were recorded and analyzed using FlowJo soft
-
ware (Version 10.00).
2.4. Chemiluminescence assay for cytokine detection
Peripheral blood from SLE patients and healthy volunteers was 
centrifuged at 4 ◦C and 3000 g for 20 min to collect the supernatant. 
According to the instructions of the assay kits (K15049D, K0082088, 
MSD), detection antibodies, wash solution, and read buffer were pre
-
pared. Standards, controls, and samples (50 μ L each) were added to the 
wells and incubated at room temperature with shaking at 700 rpm for 1 
h. Subsequently, 25–50 μ L of detection antibody was added and incu -
bated under the same conditions. Wells were washed three times with 
150 
μ L of wash buffer, and read buffer was added before measurement. 
The absorbance of each well was detected using the MSD platform. MSD 
DISCOVERY WORKBENCH analysis software (Version 4.0) was used to 
quantify multiple inflammatory cytokines, including TNF-
α , IL-6, and IL- 
1β, in each sample.
2.5. Statistical analysis
All statistical analyses were performed using R software (version 
4.0.2). P-values were calculated using the Wilcoxon test. In the inter -
pretation of results, a two-tailed P-value less than 0.05 was considered 
statistically significant. The P-values are represented as follows: *P <
0.05; **P < 0.01; ***P < 0.001; ns: P > 0.05.In addition, a HolmBon-
ferroni multiple comparisons adjustment was applied to the calculation 
of all diversity and sharing metrics in order to avoid a statistically sig -
nificant difference between groups as a result of the error rate of 
occurrence (FDR).
3. Results
3.1. Result 1: Single-cell landscape of peripheral blood in SLE
Using the 10×Genomics platform, we performed scRNA-seq analysis 
on peripheral blood samples from 7 SLE patients (SLE group) and 6 
healthy volunteers (NC group). We captured 38,201 cells from the NC 
group and 33,728 cells from the SLE group. Dimensionality reduction 
was performed using PCA, and unbiased clustering of cells was con -
ducted based on the SNN clustering algorithm. The cell subpopulations 
obtained through clustering analysis were annotated using the singleR 
package. These cells were ultimately identified and categorized into 
different immune cell types, including CD4+T, CD8+ T, CD4+ CD8+ T, 
ProT, NK, Monocytes, DCs, Plasma B, B, and Mgk cells, totaling 10 
subpopulations (
Fig. 1A).
To validate the annotation of cell subpopulations, we performed 
bidirectional hierarchical clustering of genes and samples, analyzing the 
expression heatmap of annotated marker genes in each cell subpopula-
tion (Fig. 1C). Marker genes for each cell group are shown in Fig. 1D-E, 
with MAL used to identify CD4 + CD8 + T cells, CCL5 for CD8 + T cells, 
AQP3 for CD4+ T cells, UGHM for B cells, CD68 for Monocytes, KLRF1 
for NK cells, MKI67 for ProT cells, FCER1A for DCs, and MZB1 for 
Plasma B cells. These markers were significantly upregulated in their 
respective subpopulations and weakly expressed in other cell groups.
Additionally, we compared the proportions of immune cells between 
the two groups (Fig. 1B). Compared to the NC group, the SLE group 
exhibited a decrease in the proportion of B cells and CD4 + CD8+ T cells, 
while the proportions of DCs, Monocytes, Plasma B, and ProT cells 
increased. Notably, the differences in the numbers of Monocytes and 
ProT cells were the most pronounced.

4. Discussion
Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is an autoimmune disease that 
affects multiple organs and systems. The early symptoms are often 
inconspicuous, and the timing of diagnosis significantly influences pa -
tient prognosis [18]. Therefore, identifying early diagnostic targets is a 
primary focus of current research [5]. The pathogenesis is related to 
increased apoptosis and defects in apoptotic body uptake. Studies have 
shown that the apoptosis rate of peripheral blood mononuclear cells 
(PBMCs) in active SLE patients is 30 %. A comprehensive survey of 
circulating immune cells in SLE is still incomplete [19]. Thus, studying 
the types and states of circulating immune cells in SLE patients is crucial 
for understanding the disease mechanism and identifying early diag
-
nostic targets [ 10].We analyzed differentially expressed genes (DEGs) 
and enriched pathways in circulating immune cells of SLE patients 
compared to healthy volunteers. It is generally believed that monocytes 
are the effector cells responsible for the inflammation of tissues associ
-
ated with the disease, and are able to sense the central node of the pro- 
inflammatory environment. In monocytes, the expression levels of IRF1, 
IFITM3, S100A9, CCL4, and CXCL8 were significantly upregulated. 
IRF1, a member of the transcription factor family regulating host de
-
fense, acts as an intracellular mediator of interferon, and its functional 
and gene expression abnormalities are closely related to the pathogen
-
esis of SLE. CCL4 and CXCL8 play essential roles in immune cell 
chemotaxis. CCL4/macrophage inflammatory protein-1β and CXCL8 are 
among the chemokines upregulated in SLE-associated autoimmune he
-
molytic anemia and primary autoimmune hemolytic anemia. CCL4 is 
associated with the psychological health of lupus patients, and CXCL8 
levels are elevated in the serum and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) of 
neuropsychiatric SLE patients, serving as a marker for diagnosing CNS 
involvement in SLE.
According to result 2, we learned that multiple metabolic pathways 
are altered in SLE patients, including lipids, cholesterol, and glutamine, 
and that immune cells in SLE patients usually exhibit a significant in -
crease in glycolysis and upregulation of glutamine metabolic pathways, 
which provide energy for immune cell activation, and that this meta
-
bolic reprogramming promotes inflammatory responses [ 20,21]. Lipid 
metabolism is significantly altered, as evidenced by elevated levels of 
low-density lipoprotein (LDL) and total cholesterol with concomitant 
decreases in high-density lipoprotein (HDL) levels [22 ]. Our study 
provides a metabolic map for further exploration of the interaction be -
tween metabolic disorders and immune regulation in the future to 
modulate metabolic therapy for SLE.
Different cell types can promote the proliferation of autoimmune 
diseases through different signaling pathways. Additionally, the regu -
lation of signal transduction and cellular communication between cell 
subpopulations is a key process in inflammation and immunity [ 23]. 
Disease onset is often associated with certain cellular interactions; thus, 
we analyzed the differences in cell interactions in the SLE group. Our 
study found that TNF, BAFF, MIF, RESISTIN, GALECTIN, and ANNEXIN 
pathways in monocytes had higher interaction intensity in the SLE group 
than in the NC group. BAFF, a B-cell activating factor, suggests that SLE 
monocytes might participate in B-cell activation through cell in
-
teractions, promoting immune responses. BAFF signaling also interacts 
with toll-like receptor-mediated signals, enhancing immune responses. 
TNF, an inflammatory mediator, may play a significant role in the sus
-
tained inflammatory response in affected organs [ 24]. TNF plays an 
inflammatory role in SLE organ disease and is closely related to disease 
activity [25]. MIF (macrophage migration inhibitory factor) is a cyto -
kine associated with the pathogenesis of autoimmune inflammatory 
diseases [
26], regulating glucocorticoid-mediated immunosuppression 
and cell survival signaling. Targeting MIF for treating autoimmune 
diseases has entered early clinical trials [27]. The GALECTIN signaling 
pathway accelerates the progression of autoimmune diseases to a certain 
extent and also affects the activation of the MAPK/ERK signaling 
pathway [28].
IRF1 plays a pivotal role in activating the type I interferon (IFN) 
pathway, which in turn promotes the secretion of various inflammatory 
cytokines, leading to immune system overactivation [29
]. In this study, 
we observed significantly elevated expression levels of IRF1 in mono -
cytes from patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), alongside 
a marked enrichment of inflammation-related pathways in these cells. 
To further investigate, we measured several inflammatory factors, 
including IFN-γ, TNF- α , and IL-6, which are secreted by monocytes and 
found to be significantly upregulated in the serum of SLE patients. These 
cytokines play crucial roles in both inflammation and apoptosis [
30].
IFN-γ activates several signaling pathways, including STAT1/STAT3 
and NF-κB, thereby promoting the transcription of pro-inflammatory 
cytokines such as IFN-γ, IL-6, IL-8, and MCP-1 [31]. Additionally, SLE 
is associated with an imbalance between effector and regulatory CD4+T 
cells, which is tightly regulated by IL-2. This regulatory function of IL-2 
is often impaired in SLE patients, leading to immune dysregulation [32]. 
Furthermore, IL-10 may be involved in B cell hyperactivity and the 
production of autoantibodies in SLE [33
]. IL-1β is implicated in both 
acute and chronic inflammation in SLE, contributing to vascular endo -
thelial injury, joint inflammation, and damage to other organs, and may 
also lead to lupus nephritis [ 34]. TNF-α , primarily produced by mac -
rophages, lymphocytes, mast cells, eosinophils, and natural killer (NK) 
cells, plays a central role in immune suppression and inflammation [
35]. 
Elevated serum levels of TNF-α in SLE patients are associated with dis-
ease activity and a range of systemic manifestations, including cardio -
vascular diseases and lupus nephritis. IL-6 plays a critical role in SLE by 
promoting immune cell activation, proliferation, differentiation, and 
cytokine secretion [
36]. It works synergistically with IL-1β and TNF-α to 
enhance inflammatory responses. IL-6, IL-10, and TNF- α have been 
identified as potential biomarkers for assessing disease activity and 
severity in SLE [37]. Overall, IRF1 is closely associated with the onset 
and progression of SLE. Inhibition of IRF1 expression or its downstream 
signaling pathways may represent a promising therapeutic strategy for 
SLE [38]. By further elucidating the mechanisms underlying IRF1’s role 
in SLE, targeting the interferon signaling pathway could reduce the 
immune system’s attack on self-tissues, potentially alleviating clinical 
symptoms and offering a novel approach for the treatment of SLE and 
other autoimmune diseases [29].
